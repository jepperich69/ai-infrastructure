This proposal claims to shift the presentation from a technical division-level talk to a "strategic" leadergroup discussion. It fails fundamentally at this task. Compressing technical slides and merely changing the title from "what you can do" to "what leaders can do" does not constitute a strategy. You are still presenting an operational tutorial disguised as a leadership briefing. 

Here are the critical failure modes in the current plan:

**1. The "N=1" Productivity Fallacy (Slide 2)**
Claiming a "50% productivity gain" based purely on the presenter's anecdotal experience is academically reckless and will be immediately dismantled by a competent leadergroup. 50% of what? Time to draft? Total publications? If you are generating papers 50% faster but the quality is derivative, the university's reputation suffers. Furthermore, the presenter operates a highly customized, custom-coded AI infrastructure. Extrapolating this to the average faculty member without acknowledging the massive skill gap and required infrastructure investment is dangerously optimistic.

**2. The Jargon Trap (Suggestion #5)**
Why are we introducing terms like "pipeline consensus," "convergence forum," and "SAD" (Single-Agent Debate) to leadership? They do not care about your specific PowerShell scripts or local agent architectures. They care about capabilities, resource costs, and risks. Presenting this esoteric taxonomy will alienate the room. You must abstract this into business capabilities: e.g., "Automated Peer Review," "Cross-Project Knowledge Synthesis."

**3. The Abstraction Failure of "Structure" (Suggestion #4)**
Telling leaders that "file-structure is key" and "logging is non-trivial" is entirely in the weeds. The strategic equivalent is **Data Sovereignty, Compliance, and Institutional Memory**. The argument shouldn't be about folder hygiene; it must be about ensuring DTU retains the intellectual property (IP) and data lineage of AI-assisted research, rather than leaking it to black-box commercial LLMs.

**4. The Missing Strategic "Ask" (Suggestion #7)**
A presentation to leadership without a concrete decision matrix is a waste of their time. The proposal lacks any strategic requests. Are we asking for funding for a centralized GPU cluster? Are we requesting policy updates on AI in PhD assessments? Are we asking for engineering headcount to maintain this infrastructure? Without this, the presentation is just a "show and tell."

**5. The "Teaching/Admin" Dismissal Risk (Suggestion #1)**
Briefly mentioning teaching and admin on Slide 2 only to immediately dismiss them is weak framing. Leaders manage the whole organization. If you state there is "huge potential" in admin but refuse to discuss it, you leave the most obvious ROI on the table. If research is the focus, frame it strategically: Research is the *vanguard* or *sandbox* for developing the rigorous AI compliance and workflows that will eventually scale to admin and teaching.

The proposed changes fail to elevate the presentation to a strategic level, remaining bogged down in operational details and anecdotal claims. The 50% productivity claim is unsupported and risky without defining quality metrics. The inclusion of technical jargon (SAD, convergence forums) will alienate leadership, who need to hear about capabilities and risks, not script architectures. The discussion of "file structure" must be elevated to institutional IP and compliance. Most critically, the proposal lacks a clear strategic "ask" (e.g., funding, policy changes) required for a leadergroup meeting, and the dismissal of teaching/admin potential is poorly framed.
