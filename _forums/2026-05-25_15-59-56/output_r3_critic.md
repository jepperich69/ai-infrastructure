# R3 Critic Output

## Adversarial Contribution

Four failure modes in the current settled/proposed state that need resolution before implementation.

---

### Failure 1: The "Where most groups will be in 12 months" slide is a credibility bomb

The settled plan drops the alertblock content and replaces it with a standalone forecast slide. This is the most dangerous item in the deck for a leadership audience.

Group leaders and department heads will immediately ask: "Based on what?" A prediction about where "most groups" will be in 12 months is either (a) backed by DTU-specific survey data, which doesn't exist yet, or (b) the speaker's educated guess dressed up as a forecast. Both outcomes are bad. If the forecast is wrong — and 12-month AI forecasts have a poor track record — it damages the credibility of everything that preceded it.

The fix is not to drop the slide but to reframe its claim type. "Where most groups will be in 12 months" (forecast) → "Where we want most groups to be in 12 months" (strategic aspiration). The content stays identical; the epistemic claim changes from prediction to leadership objective. This is actually stronger for a leadership deck: it invites the room to own the target rather than waiting to see if the forecast lands.

**Required action:** Change slide claim type from forecast to aspiration.

---

### Failure 2: #2b proposed title is still infrastructure-framed, violating the settled top-down inversion principle

The settled consensus is "strategic imperative first, infrastructure second throughout." The proposed #2b title — "DTU's AI Choices: In-House vs. Cloud Agents" — is an infrastructure-framing title. "In-House vs. Cloud" is a technical-architecture distinction. Leadership audiences hear data-center decisions, not governance decisions.

The actual content of this slide (based on the original brief) is about what tools DTU has approved, what governance choices have been made, and what this means for researchers. That is a governance/data-sovereignty frame, not an infrastructure frame.

The Realist's counter-proposal improved on the original but didn't fully invert. A title consistent with the settled principle: "DTU's AI Policy: What's Approved and Why It Matters." This frames the slide as a decision that has already been made at a higher level — exactly the right register for leaders who need to align their groups with institutional policy, not debate the technical merits.

**Required action:** Move #2b title proposal to active arena; propose new framing.

---

### Failure 3: "Published figures are floors not ceilings" will be read as advocacy, not evidence

The settled productivity slide framing includes "published figures are floors not ceilings." This phrasing is adversarial toward the existing evidence base and will be immediately flagged by any leader who has read McKinsey or BCG reports.

The causal mechanism the speaker means to invoke — that old-tool studies understate current potential — is valid and defensible. But "floors not ceilings" is the interpretation, not the argument. A skeptical leader hears: "Ignore the lower published numbers, trust my higher personal estimate." That is the opposite of the intended effect.

Fix: replace "floors not ceilings" with the actual argument. "Caveat: most published studies used agents 1-2 years old — the gap since then is larger than any previous 2-year period in AI development." This says the same thing but gives the audience a reasoning chain they can evaluate, rather than an assertion they must accept.

**Required action:** Revise productivity slide framing language.

---

### Failure 4: Mentioning teaching and admin in the scope slide opens more doors than it closes

The scope slide is meant to narrow focus to research. The parking lot holds the "door-closing" sentence for teaching and admin. But naming teaching and admin in a leadergroup context is structurally problematic regardless of wording.

Leaders govern teaching and admin. When a presenter says "we won't cover teaching or admin today," a room of Group Leaders and Department Heads hears: "There are bigger AI challenges we're not equipped to address yet." This creates anxiety and distraction precisely when you want alignment.

The Realist framed this as a wording problem. It is not. It is a decision about what to mention at all. Two clean options:

- **Option A:** Drop teaching/admin from the scope slide entirely. Just say "Today: research." If asked, address verbally. No anxiety created.
- **Option B:** Include teaching/admin with a concrete next-step reference. "Teaching and admin raise different questions — we're scheduling a separate forum for Q3." This requires an actual commitment but neutralizes the anxiety.

"We're focusing on research" without either of these is the worst outcome: it mentions the problem, signals it matters, and then abandons it.

**Required action:** Force a decision on whether to mention teaching/admin at all; parking the wording defers a structural choice.
